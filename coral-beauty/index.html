Coral Beauty Angelfish Care Guide: Tank Setup, Diet & Reef Compatibility

Quick Facts

Scientific Name
Centropyge bispinosa
Care Level
Beginner-friendly — one of the hardier dwarf angelfish
Minimum Tank Size
30 gallons (114 L)
Temperament
Semi-aggressive, territorial toward similar shapes, generally calmer than flame angelfish
Diet
Omnivore (marine algae, mysis, pellet, nori)
Reef Safe
With caution — occasional LPS polyp-picking, similar to flame angelfish
Max Size
~4 inches (10 cm)
Lifespan
5-7 years with good care

The coral beauty angelfish (Centropyge bispinosa) is one of the most commonly recommended "first angelfish" in the saltwater hobby — a compact, vividly colored dwarf angel that's hardy enough for a moderately experienced beginner while still carrying the same general reef-safety caveats as its more famous cousin, the flame angelfish.

Short Answer

The coral beauty angelfish is a hardy, beginner-friendly dwarf angelfish that does well in tanks of 30 gallons or more, eats a varied omnivorous diet of algae and mysis-based foods, and is generally considered reef-safe with the same caveats as most Centropyge species — occasional LPS polyp-picking is possible but not guaranteed. Its coloration (deep purple-blue with orange-yellow highlights) and slightly smaller minimum tank size set it apart from the flame angelfish, but the two share most of their care requirements, temperament, and reef-compatibility considerations.

Appearance and Natural Range

Coral beauties reach roughly 4 inches (10 cm) at full size — in the same general size class as the flame angelfish and most other popular Centropyge species. Their base coloration is typically a rich purple-to-blue body, often with orange or yellow shading concentrated toward the head and fins, and fine vertical striping along the body. Color intensity and pattern vary considerably depending on collection locality — fish from different parts of the Indo-Pacific can look noticeably different from each other, which is part of why "coral beauty" covers a range of appearances rather than one fixed look.

In the wild, C. bispinosa has one of the widest distributions of any Centropyge species, found across the Indo-Pacific from the Red Sea and East Africa to the Pacific islands. It inhabits reef slopes and lagoons, often in areas with rubble and mixed coral growth, where — like most dwarf angelfish — it spends most of its time grazing algae and small invertebrates off the substrate and rockwork.

Tank Requirements

Tank Size

A single coral beauty needs a minimum of 30 gallons (114 liters) — somewhat smaller than the 55-gallon minimum typically recommended for a flame angelfish. This makes the coral beauty a more realistic option for keepers working with a smaller saltwater setup, though the same general principle applies: more space and more rockwork generally produces a more confident, more visible fish. In a 30-gallon tank, a coral beauty is likely to be the largest and most territorial fish present; in a larger tank, it integrates more easily alongside other peaceful-to-semi-aggressive species.

Aquascaping

As with most dwarf angelfish, dense live rock with plenty of caves and crevices is the single most important aquascaping consideration. Coral beauties are reef-slope grazers in the wild, constantly moving between cover and open rock faces, and a tank that's mostly open water with minimal structure tends to produce a fish that stays hidden and stressed. Leaving some open swimming space in the mid-to-upper water column, in addition to the rockwork, gives a settled coral beauty room to patrol once it feels secure.

Water Parameters

Parameter Target Range
Temperature 74-80°F (23-27°C)
Salinity 1.023-1.025 SG
pH 8.1-8.4
Ammonia / Nitrite 0 ppm
Nitrate <10 ppm (reef tanks), <20 ppm (fish-only)
Alkalinity 8-11 dKH

These ranges are essentially identical to those for the flame angelfish and most other common dwarf angelfish — and to typical reef tank parameters generally. Coral beauties are often described as slightly more tolerant of a maturing tank's water-quality swings than flame angels, which is part of why they're frequently suggested as an earlier addition, though a fully cycled tank with stable parameters is still the right starting point for any angelfish.

Diet and Feeding

Coral beauties are grazers by nature — in the wild they spend much of the day picking at algae films, biofilm, and small invertebrates across rock and rubble. Replicating that grazing behavior is the core of keeping one healthy and reducing the chance it samples coral polyps out of hunger or boredom.

A solid feeding routine includes:

  • Nori or dried seaweed sheets, clipped to the rockwork for continuous grazing throughout the day
  • High-quality marine angelfish pellet or flake formulated with algae and sponge content
  • Frozen mysis shrimp, enriched with a vitamin supplement, a few times per week
  • Established live rock with algae growth, which gives the fish something to forage on between feedings

Feeding 2-3 small meals daily — the same approach used for the flame angelfish — keeps a coral beauty consistently fed and is one of the more reliable ways to reduce coral-picking risk in a reef tank.

Tank Mates and Compatibility

Coral beauties are semi-aggressive and territorial, primarily toward fish with a similar body shape — other dwarf angelfish, butterflyfish, and similarly shaped reef fish. Toward most other tank mates, they're considered relatively peaceful.

Good tank mate choices:

  • Clownfish, including the common clownfish, which occupy a different niche and rarely conflict with a coral beauty
  • Peaceful damselfish such as the yellowtail damselfish, along with most gobies, blennies, and wrasses
  • Cardinalfish, including the Banggai cardinalfish — a water-column-dwelling species with minimal territorial overlap

Tank mates to avoid or approach carefully:

  • Other dwarf angelfish — including flame angelfish and other coral beauties — in tanks under 100 gallons, where territorial conflict is likely
  • Butterflyfish, including the pearlscale butterflyfish, in smaller tanks where grazing territory overlaps

On reef compatibility: Coral beauties sit in roughly the same "proceed with awareness" category as flame angelfish. Most individuals don't bother SPS or soft corals, but LPS corals with soft, exposed polyps — zoanthids, palythoa, and similar species — are the most commonly reported targets if a coral beauty does pick at coral. As with the flame angel, there's no reliable way to know in advance whether a given fish will do this, though a heavy grazing-based feeding routine measurably reduces the odds.

Common Health Issues

Coral beauties are generally considered one of the hardier dwarf angelfish, but the same issues that affect Centropyge species broadly apply:

  • Marine ich and velvet — most common in newly imported fish stressed from shipping; a 2-4 week quarantine period before adding to a display tank is the standard precaution
  • Head and lateral line erosion (HLLE) — pitting around the face, often linked to nutritional gaps; a varied, algae-rich diet is the main preventive measure
  • Prolonged hiding and not eating — most common in the first 1-2 weeks after introduction; persistent hiding past that window, especially combined with not eating, is worth investigating for water quality or tank-mate harassment issues

A coral beauty that's actively grazing, holding its color, and exploring the tank within the first couple of weeks generally settles into a long-term, low-maintenance addition to a reef or fish-only marine tank.

Quick Reference

  • Tank: 30+ gallons, fully cycled with established live rock
  • Dense rockwork with caves, plus some open swimming space
  • Salinity 1.023-1.025, temperature 74-80°F, alkalinity 8-11 dKH
  • Quarantine new arrivals 2-4 weeks before adding to display
  • Feed 2-3x daily: nori, angelfish pellet, enriched mysis
  • Avoid keeping with other dwarf angelfish in tanks under 100 gallons
  • If reef keeping: watch zoanthids and other soft-polyp LPS corals closely after introduction

Frequently Asked Questions

Is the coral beauty angelfish reef safe?

Mostly, with the same caveats that apply to most dwarf Centropyge angelfish. Coral beauties generally leave SPS and soft corals alone and spend most of their time grazing algae off rock, but a portion of individuals will occasionally pick at LPS coral polyps — zoanthids, palythoa, and similar soft-bodied polyps are the most commonly reported targets. This puts the coral beauty in a similar 'proceed with awareness' category as the flame angelfish, though anecdotally many keepers report coral beauties as somewhat less prone to coral-picking than flame angels — there's no guarantee either way for an individual fish.

What's the difference between a coral beauty and a flame angelfish?

Both are dwarf angelfish in the genus Centropyge, similar in size (around 4 inches), care requirements, and general temperament — but they look quite different and have a slightly different reputation. The flame angelfish is a vivid red-orange with bold black vertical bars. The coral beauty is typically a deep purple-blue body with orange-to-yellow highlights and finer vertical striping, though coloration varies noticeably depending on the collection locality (Indo-Pacific populations show a wide range of color forms). In terms of care, the coral beauty is often considered the slightly hardier and more beginner-friendly of the two, tolerating a smaller minimum tank size (30 gallons vs. 55 for the flame angel) — though both share the same general reef-safety caveats around LPS corals.

Can I keep a coral beauty with other angelfish?

Generally not in the same tank unless it's quite large (100+ gallons) with very different-looking species and plenty of territory. Like other Centropyge angelfish, coral beauties are territorial toward fish with a similar body shape — including other coral beauties, flame angelfish, and most other dwarf angelfish. Two coral beauties in a smaller tank will typically result in one harassing the other persistently. If you want to keep multiple dwarf angelfish, a large, heavily aquascaped tank with multiple established territories gives the best odds, but it's still a gamble rather than a reliable combination.

What does a coral beauty angelfish eat?

A varied omnivorous diet built around grazing, the same general approach as for the flame angelfish: nori or dried seaweed sheets clipped to the rock for continuous grazing, a high-quality marine angelfish pellet or flake with algae/sponge content, and frozen mysis shrimp a few times a week for protein. Feeding small amounts 2-3 times daily, plus letting the fish graze on algae growth on live rock between feedings, keeps a coral beauty well-fed and reduces the odds it turns to coral polyps out of hunger.
