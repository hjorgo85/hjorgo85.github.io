Yellowtail Damselfish Care Guide: Tank Setup, Diet & Breeding

The yellowtail damselfish (Chrysiptera parasema), sometimes called the yellowtail blue damsel, is one of the most common entry points into saltwater fishkeeping — and for good reason. It's inexpensive, strikingly colored (a deep cobalt-blue body with a bright yellow tail and dorsal/caudal fins), tolerant of the water-quality swings that happen while a new tank cycles, and genuinely reef safe. This guide covers everything you need to keep one (or a small group) healthy long-term: tank setup, water parameters, diet, tank mates, and what happens if yours decides to breed.

Appearance and Natural Range

Yellowtail damselfish are small, oval-bodied fish reaching about 3 inches (7.5 cm) at maturity. Their coloring is their main draw: an electric blue body that can appear almost purple under reef lighting, contrasted with a bright yellow tail, dorsal fin, and anal fin. Juveniles are often even more vividly colored than adults.

In the wild, C. parasema is found throughout the Western Pacific, typically in shallow lagoons and reef flats at depths of 1-15 meters, where it shelters among branching corals and rubble. This natural behavior — using rockwork as a bolt-hole when threatened — carries directly over to aquarium care: a yellowtail damselfish without adequate hiding spots will be a stressed, less colorful fish.

Tank Requirements

Tank Size

A single yellowtail damselfish can be kept comfortably in a tank as small as 20 gallons (75 liters), though they do equally well in larger systems. If you're planning to keep a small group (discussed below), aim for 50+ gallons to dilute territorial disputes.

Aquascaping

Provide plenty of live rock with caves, overhangs, and crevices. Yellowtail damselfish are most comfortable — and display their best coloration — when they have multiple bolt-holes to retreat to. In a reef tank, they'll naturally weave between coral branches, which also helps them establish (and defend) a small territory, reducing stress-driven aggression toward tankmates elsewhere in the tank.

Water Parameters

Parameter Target Range
Temperature 72-78°F (22-26°C)
Salinity 1.020-1.025 SG
pH 8.1-8.4
Ammonia / Nitrite 0 ppm
Nitrate <20 ppm (lower for reef tanks with sensitive corals)
Alkalinity 8-12 dKH

Yellowtail damselfish are among the most forgiving saltwater fish when it comes to water quality fluctuations — they're frequently used as the first fish added during tank cycling because they tolerate the ammonia/nitrite spikes of a new biological filter better than most species. This doesn't mean you should skip water testing. Use their hardiness as a buffer for your learning curve, not a reason to neglect parameters long-term — chronic poor water quality will still shorten their lifespan and dull their coloration.

Diet and Feeding

Yellowtail damselfish are unfussy omnivores. In the wild they feed on zooplankton, algae, and small invertebrates; in the aquarium, they'll readily accept:

  • High-quality marine flake food
  • Marine pellets (1-2mm, sinking or floating)
  • Frozen mysis shrimp or brine shrimp (enriched, if possible)
  • Nori/seaweed sheets clipped to the tank for grazing

Feed 1-2 times daily, offering only what they can consume within 2-3 minutes. Damselfish have hearty appetites and will beg constantly, but overfeeding contributes to nitrate spikes — a bigger long-term problem for your tank than a slightly hungry damsel.

Tank Mates and Compatibility

This is where yellowtail damselfish require the most thought. They are semi-aggressive and territorial, especially as they mature, and they're notorious for picking on fish added after them — particularly other small, similarly shaped, or similarly colored fish.

Good tank mate strategies:

  • Add damselfish last, or alongside other semi-aggressive species of similar size, so no single fish has time to establish dominant territory before others arrive.
  • Pair with clownfish (see our common clownfish guide), firefish (see our firefish guide), gobies, blennies, and larger semi-aggressive fish like tangs (in appropriately sized tanks).
  • Avoid keeping them with very passive, slow-moving fish (e.g. seahorses, pipefish) or other small damselfish species added later — both scenarios tend to end badly for the newcomer.
  • If you're considering other Chrysiptera damsels like the azure damselfish or blue devil damselfish, or a much larger species like the domino damselfish, add them simultaneously to a large tank with divided territories rather than sequentially — mixing similarly colored damsels added at different times is one of the most common causes of ongoing aggression.

Keeping a group: If you want multiple yellowtail damselfish, the established advice is to add 4 or more at once into a tank with abundant rockwork. This disperses aggression across the group rather than concentrating it on one or two individuals. Adding them in twos or threes, or sequentially over time, tends to produce one dominant fish that harasses the rest.

Breeding in the Home Aquarium

Yellowtail damselfish are one of the few marine fish that will readily spawn in a standard home reef tank without special intervention — a notable difference from most saltwater species, which require dedicated breeding setups.

What to expect:

  1. A bonded pair will select a flat surface — typically a shaded patch of rock or the underside of a rock ledge — and the male will clean it obsessively for days before spawning.
  2. The female lays a patch of small purple-to-grey eggs, which the male fertilizes and then guards aggressively, fanning them with his fins to keep them oxygenated and free of debris.
  3. Eggs typically hatch 5-7 days after spawning, usually after dark. Larvae are pelagic and will be swept into the water column — in a reef tank with a sump, most will be lost to the overflow/return pumps unless you take steps to capture them (e.g., covering intakes, or running the tank "wet/dry" overnight with pumps off).
  4. If you want to attempt raising the fry, you'll need a separate larval-rearing tank with green water (phytoplankton) and rotifers as first food — this is an advanced project, not something that happens passively in a display tank.

Even if you have no interest in breeding, it's worth knowing this behavior exists: a male yellowtail damselfish guarding eggs can become noticeably more aggressive toward anything approaching "his" patch of rock, including your hand during maintenance.

Common Health Issues

Yellowtail damselfish are hardy and rarely the first fish to show signs of disease — but they're not immune. Watch for:

  • Marine ich (Cryptocaryon irritans) — white salt-like spots on the body and fins. Damsels can carry it without obvious symptoms while other fish in the tank show signs.
  • Fin rot — usually secondary to stress (often from aggression toward other fish) or poor water quality.
  • Brooklynella — less common in damsels than in clownfish, but possible in newly imported stock.

Quarantining new fish for 2-4 weeks before adding them to your main display remains the single most effective way to avoid introducing disease — including from a seemingly bulletproof yellowtail damselfish.

Quick Setup Checklist

  • Tank: 20+ gallons, established (cycled) before adding fish
  • Live rock with multiple caves/overhangs
  • Salinity 1.020-1.025, temperature 72-78°F
  • Protein skimmer or equivalent filtration + water movement (powerhead)
  • Marine flake/pellet + occasional frozen mysis
  • Plan tank-mate order: add damsels last, or in a group of 4+ simultaneously

Frequently Asked Questions

How many yellowtail damselfish can I keep together?

A single yellowtail damselfish is the safest choice in tanks under 40 gallons. If you want a small group, add 4-5 of them simultaneously to a tank of 50+ gallons with plenty of rockwork — adding them one at a time, or in small numbers, tends to result in the established fish bullying newcomers.

Are yellowtail damselfish reef safe?

Yes. Yellowtail damselfish won't bother corals, clams, or other sessile invertebrates, making them a solid choice for reef tanks. Their only compatibility concern is with other fish, not with corals.

Why is my yellowtail damselfish turning dark or losing color?

Temporary darkening is often a stress or aggression response (common during tank introductions or territorial disputes) and usually resolves within a few days. Persistent color loss combined with clamped fins, rapid breathing, or loss of appetite can indicate poor water quality or disease — test your water parameters first.

Do yellowtail damselfish need a sump or just a hang-on-back filter?

Either works. Yellowtail damselfish are hardy and tolerate a range of filtration setups, including HOB (hang-on-back) filters with a protein skimmer, canister filters, or a full sump system. What matters more is stable water parameters and adequate water movement (a powerhead helps in tanks without a sump return).
