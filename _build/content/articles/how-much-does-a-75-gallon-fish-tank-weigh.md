A 75-gallon tank is a popular "stepping up" size — big enough for serious stocking options, small enough to fit through most doorways. What's easy to underestimate is how much heavier the *finished* tank is compared to "75 gallons of water," once everything else is added in.

## Direct Answer: Roughly 850-1000+ lbs All In

**A fully set-up 75-gallon tank typically weighs somewhere around 850-1000+ lbs total**, combining: roughly **625 lbs of water** (freshwater, at about 8.34 lbs/gallon — slightly more for saltwater), roughly **130-150 lbs for the empty glass tank** itself, and roughly **100-200+ lbs of substrate, rock, and decor** depending on depth and hardscape. **Water dominates the total**, but the other components together are far from negligible — easily 200-350+ lbs combined depending on setup. For most ground-floor placements on an appropriate stand, this is within normal residential floor capacity, though upper floors and older structures are worth a moment's thought.

## Breaking Down the Total

**Water**: Freshwater is roughly **8.34 lbs per gallon**, so 75 gallons ≈ **625 lbs**. Saltwater is slightly denser — roughly **8.5-8.6 lbs/gallon**, or about **640 lbs** for 75 gallons. The difference (~15 lbs) is small relative to the whole.

**The tank itself**: An empty 75-gallon glass tank commonly weighs roughly **130-150 lbs**, depending on glass thickness and construction (acrylic tanks of the same volume are often lighter, though less common at this size).

**Substrate**: This scales with footprint and depth. Our [guide to sand quantity for a 20-gallon tank](/how-much-sand-for-a-20-gallon-tank/) covers the calculation at a smaller scale — a 75-gallon tank has roughly 3-4x the footprint of a 20-gallon, so a few inches of substrate can put this component in the **75-150+ lb** range. A [laterite layer](/aquarium-laterite/) or a [soil base under a sand cap](/potting-soil-aquariums/) adds to this further, though usually modestly compared to the main substrate layer.

**Rock and decor**: Highly variable — a sparsely decorated tank adds relatively little here, while a heavily rockscaped reef tank (live rock is notably dense) or a tank with large pieces of driftwood and stone can add a substantial amount on its own.

## Floor and Stand Considerations

For a **ground-floor placement on a properly rated stand**, a 75-gallon tank is a very common setup and generally within what residential floors are designed to support — this isn't a tank size that typically raises structural alarm bells. A few things still worth keeping in mind:

- **How the load is distributed** — a stand's legs concentrate weight at specific points rather than spreading it evenly, which matters more for delicate flooring
- **Upper-floor placement** — span and joist considerations become more relevant than on a slab/ground floor
- **Older homes** — less certainty about floor condition can be worth a quick look, especially for upper floors

None of this is a reason to avoid a 75-gallon tank — it's a reason to give the **stand and placement** a bit of thought, particularly **positioning over joists** for upper-floor setups.
