Fish Facts & FAQs: How Fish Actually Work

Some of the most common fish-related questions aren't about a specific species or tank setup — they're basic 'how does this actually work' questions about fish themselves. This section answers those questions directly, with the biology and physics behind them, so the explanations hold up beyond a one-line answer.

What This Section Covers

These are the questions that come up when you're just sitting and watching your tank — why a fish does what it does, what it can actually perceive, how its body works compared to ours. Each guide gives a direct, biology-based answer rather than a one-line guess, and connects back to practical fishkeeping considerations where relevant.

Fish Facts & FAQs

Why Can't Fish Breathe Air? How Gills Actually Work

Air contains roughly 20 times more oxygen than water, yet most fish can't use it — their gills are built around a structure that only works when submerged.
Fish Facts & FAQs

Do Fish Get Cold? How Temperature Actually Affects Them

Fish are largely ectothermic, meaning their body temperature follows the water's — so 'cold' isn't felt the way it is by mammals, but temperature still drives metabolism, immunity, and stress.
Fish Facts & FAQs

Do Fish Float When They Die? What Actually Happens

A dead fish often floats, but usually not immediately — gas buildup from decomposition and the swim bladder is the main cause, which is why a missing fish may be sunk and out of sight at first.
Fish Facts & FAQs

Can Fish Get Drunk? What Happens When Substances Enter Their Water

Fish absorb dissolved substances directly through their gills, which is why alcohol, household chemicals, and medications can all have outsized effects on fish at concentrations that would barely register for land animals.
Fish Facts & FAQs

What Do Fish See From Inside the Tank? Fish Vision Explained

A fish looking up through the water's surface sees a compressed circular window onto the world above, surrounded by a mirror-like reflection of the tank's own interior — a real optical effect, not a metaphor.

Why Do Fish Jump Out of the Water? Causes and How to Prevent It

Wild fish jump to escape predators, catch insect prey, or move between bodies of water. In a tank, jumping is usually a startle response — and it's a real safety hazard without a lid.
Fish Facts & FAQs

Can Saltwater Fish Live in Freshwater? Osmoregulation Explained

A fish's body is constantly fighting to keep the right balance of water and salt inside its cells. Marine and freshwater fish fight that battle in opposite directions — which is why most can't simply swap environments.

What Does a Small Fish Eat? Diet Basics for Tiny Fish and Fry

A small fish's diet is mostly dictated by its mouth size — small mouths mean small food, whether that's zooplankton in the wild or crushed flakes and infusoria in a tank.

How Do Fish Get in Ponds? The Surprisingly Mundane Answer

Fish 'appearing' in a pond that was never stocked looks like a mystery, but the real explanations — birds, floods, deliberate stocking, hitchhiking eggs — are all fairly ordinary.

Does Fishing Hurt Fish? What the Science Says About Pain in Fish

Fish have nociceptors — nerve receptors that detect potentially damaging stimuli — but whether that adds up to 'pain' in the way mammals experience it is a genuinely open scientific question.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why do these questions matter for someone keeping fish, not just out of curiosity?

A lot of practical fishkeeping decisions trace back to these basics. Understanding why fish can't breathe air explains why surface gasping is a water-quality signal worth acting on. Understanding how temperature affects fish explains why sudden temperature swings during water changes matter more than people expect. Even something like what a fish sees from inside the tank connects to real considerations about tank placement and reducing stress. The biology isn't just trivia — it's the reasoning behind a lot of the 'best practices' that get repeated without always being explained.

Are these questions answered the same way for every fish species?

The underlying mechanisms — how gills work, ectothermic temperature regulation, gill-based absorption of dissolved substances — are broadly shared across fish, but specifics vary by species, sometimes significantly. Labyrinth fish like bettas are a notable exception to the 'gills only' respiration pattern, for example. Where species-specific detail matters, these guides point to the relevant species profiles — but the core explanations here are about the general biology that applies across the hobby, which is the part that's often missing from quick one-line answers elsewhere.
