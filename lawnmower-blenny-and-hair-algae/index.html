Lawnmower Blenny and Hair Algae: What It Eats, and What It Won't

Quick Facts

Scientific Name
Salarias fasciatus
Care Level
Moderate — easy to feed if algae is present, harder to feed if it isn't
Minimum Tank Size
30 gallons (114 L), with established algae growth
Diet
Primarily algae grazer — turf/hair algae and diatoms, supplemented with herbivore foods
Temperament
Generally peaceful, can be territorial with other bottom-dwelling blennies
Reef Safety
Generally reef safe — won't bother corals or inverts, but will graze coralline algae lightly
Starvation Risk
Real concern in tanks with little to no algae growth — needs ongoing food source or supplementation
Max Size
Around 5 inches (12-13 cm)

Few fish in the saltwater hobby have a reputation as specific as the lawnmower blenny (Salarias fasciatus) — bought, almost without exception, to deal with hair or turf algae. That reputation is largely earned: this is a genuine algae grazer, not a fish that's marketed as one but doesn't really eat much of it. The catch isn't whether it eats algae — it's what happens once it's done a good job.

Direct Answer: A Genuine Algae Grazer, With Caveats

The lawnmower blenny actively grazes turf algae, hair algae, and diatoms off rock and other surfaces, using a specialized mouth adapted for scraping. In a tank with substantial nuisance algae, it can make a visible difference. The caveats are less about whether it works and more about sustainability: it needs an ongoing supply of algae (or supplemental feeding) to stay healthy, and it doesn't change the light or nutrient conditions that are actually driving algae growth — covered generally in our algae guide and, for reef-specific algae types, our 17 reef plants and algae guide.

What It Eats and Tank Requirements

Turf algae and hair algae are the primary targets, along with diatoms (the brown film common in newer tanks). A 30-gallon tank is often cited as a practical minimum, but the more meaningful requirement is algae-covered surface area — live rock with established turf algae growth gives the fish both habitat and an ongoing food source. A bare-bottom tank or one with minimal rock, regardless of gallon count, is a poor fit.

The Starvation Risk in a "Too Clean" Tank

This is the central tension with lawnmower blennies, and it's worth understanding before buying one: the fish is typically purchased because a tank has an algae problem, but the goal of having it is to reduce that algae — which, if successful, can leave the fish without enough food. Signs of an underfed lawnmower blenny include a visibly sunken belly, reduced activity, and gradual weight loss. The fix is supplemental feeding — algae sheets (nori), herbivore-formulated pellets or flakes — once algae levels in the tank drop. This isn't a failure of care; it's simply that the food source the fish initially relied on (excess algae) has been successfully reduced, and ongoing feeding fills that gap.

Reef Safety and Tank Mates

Lawnmower blennies are widely considered reef safe — they don't target coral tissue or most invertebrates, and are a common addition to reef tanks specifically for algae control. One minor point worth knowing: they will graze on coralline algae to some degree as part of general grazing behavior, though this is typically minor and not considered a significant drawback given the benefit on turf/hair algae. Territorial behavior toward other bottom-dwelling blennies or similarly-shaped fish is possible, but lawnmower blennies are generally peaceful toward fish that don't compete for the same niche.

Algae Eaters Don't Fix the Root Cause

It's worth repeating because it's the single most common misunderstanding with this fish (and with algae eaters generally, including freshwater species like the American flagfish): grazers manage visible algae, but the actual drivers — light duration/intensity and nutrient levels (nitrate, phosphate) — are what determine how much algae grows in the first place. A lawnmower blenny in a tank with an unaddressed light or nutrient imbalance will graze constantly without the algae ever meaningfully decreasing, simply because regrowth keeps pace with grazing. Addressing the underlying cause, covered in our algae guide, is what actually resolves an algae problem — the lawnmower blenny is a helpful part of an otherwise-balanced tank, not a substitute for balance.

Quick Reference

  • Lawnmower blennies genuinely graze turf algae, hair algae, and diatoms
  • Needs substantial algae-covered rock surface, not just tank volume
  • Starvation risk increases as algae is successfully reduced — watch for sunken belly
  • Supplement with nori/herbivore foods once algae levels drop
  • Generally reef safe, with minor coralline algae grazing as a normal behavior
  • Doesn't address the light/nutrient causes of algae — a helper, not a fix
  • Can be territorial with other bottom-dwelling blennies

Frequently Asked Questions

Will a lawnmower blenny get rid of my hair algae problem?

It will genuinely eat hair and turf algae — that part of its reputation is accurate. But it won't address why the algae is growing, which is almost always a light or nutrient issue (covered in our algae guide). A lawnmower blenny in a tank with an ongoing algae-driving imbalance will graze visible growth, but new algae will keep growing to replace it, often faster than one fish can keep up with in a heavily affected tank. Think of it as a maintenance helper for a tank that's otherwise being managed correctly, not a fix for an underlying problem.

What happens if my tank doesn't have enough algae for a lawnmower blenny?

This is the most common long-term issue with this fish, and it's somewhat paradoxical: lawnmower blennies are often bought specifically because a tank has an algae problem, but once that algae is brought under control (which is the actual goal), the fish can be left without enough food. Signs of this include a sunken-in belly, reduced activity, and weight loss over time. If algae levels drop significantly, supplemental feeding with herbivore-targeted foods (algae sheets/nori, herbivore pellets) becomes necessary — this isn't optional 'extra' feeding, it's filling a gap that the tank itself used to fill.

Is a lawnmower blenny reef safe?

Generally yes — lawnmower blennies don't target corals or most invertebrates and are commonly kept in reef tanks specifically for their algae-grazing behavior. One minor consideration: they will graze on coralline algae somewhat as part of general grazing, though this is typically light and not considered a significant problem compared to the benefit of turf algae control. Our coralline algae guide covers what normal coralline growth and coloration looks like if you want a baseline for comparison.

How big a tank does a lawnmower blenny need?

30 gallons is a commonly cited minimum, but the more important factor than raw gallons is how much algae-covered surface area (typically live rock) the tank has — since that's both habitat and food source. A 30-gallon tank with substantial algae-covered rock supports a lawnmower blenny better than a larger tank with bare or recently-cleaned rock. This ties back to the starvation consideration: tank size matters less than ongoing food availability.
