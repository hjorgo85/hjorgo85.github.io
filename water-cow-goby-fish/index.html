Cow Goby (Amblygobius phalaena) Care Guide: Tank Size, Sand Bed & Diet

By Hektor Jorgo Published October 10, 2022 Updated June 13, 2026 5 min read

Quick Facts

Scientific Name
Amblygobius phalaena
Common Names
Cow goby, banded goby, bridled goby
Care Level
Easy to moderate — feeding depends on sand bed quality
Minimum Tank Size
30 gallons (115 L) with a fine sand bed
Temperament
Peaceful toward other species; can be territorial toward other gobies
Diet
Omnivore — sifts sand for small invertebrates and detritus, plus prepared foods
Reef Safe
Yes
Max Size
Up to 5.5 inches (14 cm)

Sometimes sold under the name "cow goby" for its mottled brown-and-cream markings — and also known as the banded or bridled goby — Amblygobius phalaena is a peaceful, reef-safe fish that spends most of its day doing something genuinely useful: continuously sifting the sand bed for food, which keeps the substrate aerated and helps process detritus. It's an easy fish to recommend on temperament and reef compatibility, but its core feeding behavior makes the sand bed itself one of the most important "equipment" decisions for keeping it well.

Appearance and Natural Range

The cow goby has a stocky, elongated body in mottled shades of brown, tan, and cream, often with a horizontal stripe or row of darker blotches running along the sides — the irregular, patchy pattern that gives rise to the "cow" comparison. Adults reach up to about 5.5 inches (14 cm), making it one of the larger commonly kept gobies, though its body shape and bottom-dwelling habits mean it doesn't need the open swimming space a fish like the blue hippo tang requires.

A. phalaena is found across the Indo-Pacific, typically in shallow lagoons and reef flats with sandy or rubble substrate — habitats that directly shape its core behavior in the aquarium, where it spends the majority of its time at or just above the substrate, picking through sand.

The cow goby is also a useful reference point for the goby family generally — its stocky body, bottom-dwelling habits, and substrate-sifting behavior are fairly representative of what "goby" means across the huge number of species in this family, covered in more general terms in our overview of gobies.

Tank Requirements

Tank Size

A minimum of 30 gallons is generally recommended, less because of the fish's own size requirements and more because of the sand bed area needed to support its natural sifting behavior. A 30-gallon tank with a generous, fine sand bed across most of the footprint serves this species better than a larger tank with minimal sand area.

Aquascaping — The Sand Bed Is the Priority

This is the section that matters most for this species. A. phalaena needs a fine sand bed at least 2-3 inches deep across a meaningful portion of the tank floor — not a thin dusting, and not crushed coral, which is too coarse for the sifting behavior this fish relies on for both feeding and natural behavior.

Rockwork should leave open sand areas rather than covering the entire bottom — a common aquascaping approach that prioritizes rock coverage for corals can inadvertently leave a sand-sifting goby with very little usable substrate. If your tank design is rock-dominant, this is a fish to reconsider, or to plan sand areas around specifically.

Water Parameters

Parameter Target Range
Temperature 75-82°F (24-28°C)
Salinity 1.023-1.025 SG
pH 8.1-8.4
Ammonia / Nitrite 0 ppm
Nitrate <10 ppm (reef tanks)
Alkalinity 8-11 dKH

Standard reef parameters apply — as with most fish on this site, keeping specific gravity stable matters as much for the tank's overall health as for this species specifically.

Diet and Feeding

In the wild and in a well-established tank, A. phalaena spends much of the day sifting sand through its gills, filtering out copepods, small worms, and other microfauna, along with detritus and organic matter. This natural foraging is a major part of its diet — but in a newer tank, or one with a less biologically rich sand bed, natural food sources may not be sufficient on their own.

A complete feeding approach includes:

  • Established sand bed with natural microfauna — the foundation, and a major reason this species does better in mature tanks than brand-new setups
  • Sinking pellets or frozen foods (mysis, finely chopped meaty foods) offered near the substrate, where this bottom-dwelling fish will find them more readily than food that stays in the water column
  • Feeding 1-2 times daily, watching for the fish to actively forage — a cow goby that stops sifting and shows reduced body weight over time may not be getting enough from the sand bed alone and needs more direct feeding

Tank Mates and Compatibility

Cow gobies are peaceful toward other species and make good additions to a community reef tank, with a couple of specific considerations:

Good tank mates:

  • Common clownfish, cardinalfish, and other peaceful mid-water fish that don't compete for sand-bed territory
  • Snails, hermit crabs, and other cleanup crew invertebrates — no predation risk, unlike the caveat that applies to the chain link moray eel
  • Wrasses like the melanurus wrasse, which occupy a different feeding niche (active hunting vs. sand sifting) despite some overlap in habitat use

Considerations:

  • Other gobies of the same or similar species — territorial conflict over sand-bed area is the main compatibility risk with this species, particularly in smaller tanks
  • Aggressive or boisterous tankmates that might out-compete a generally unhurried, methodical forager for food

Quick Setup Checklist

  • Tank: 30+ gallons with a substantial fine sand bed area (2-3+ inches deep)
  • Avoid crushed coral or very thin sand layers — sifting behavior requires fine, deep sand
  • Leave open sand areas in aquascaping rather than covering the entire bottom with rock
  • Salinity 1.023-1.025, temperature 75-82°F, standard reef parameters
  • Allow the tank to mature before adding — established sand bed microfauna supports natural feeding
  • Supplement with sinking pellets/frozen foods 1-2x daily, especially in newer tanks
  • Avoid housing with other gobies of the same/similar species in smaller tanks
  • Compatible with cleanup crew invertebrates — no predation risk

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is Amblygobius phalaena called the cow goby?

The 'cow goby' name comes from its mottled brown-and-cream coloration, with irregular patches that some keepers compare to cow markings — similar to how other animals get common names based on pattern resemblance rather than behavior. It's also commonly called the banded goby or bridled goby, referring to the same or similar markings, and all of these names typically refer to Amblygobius phalaena.

Does a cow goby need a sand bed?

Yes, and this is the single most important setup consideration for this species. Amblygobius phalaena is a dedicated sand-sifter, constantly passing mouthfuls of substrate through its gills to filter out small invertebrates, detritus, and organic matter — both as its primary feeding behavior and as a natural way of keeping the sand bed aerated and clean. A tank with bare bottom, crushed coral, or a very thin sand layer doesn't support this behavior and can leave the fish without an adequate natural food source.

Will a cow goby eat my reef tank's cleanup crew?

No — cow gobies sift sand for microfauna (copepods, small worms, etc.) and detritus, which is a different scale and behavior from the predation seen in fish like the chain link moray eel. They're broadly compatible with snails, hermit crabs, and similar cleanup crew members, and are themselves considered part of a 'sand bed cleanup crew' role in many reef tanks.

Can I keep more than one cow goby together?

It depends on the tank. Amblygobius phalaena can be territorial toward other individuals of its own species (and sometimes similar-looking gobies) in smaller tanks, where there isn't enough sand bed area for each fish to establish its own foraging territory. In larger tanks (75+ gallons) with ample sand bed area, a mated pair or small group is sometimes kept successfully, but a single individual is the safer default for most home aquariums.
