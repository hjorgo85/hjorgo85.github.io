How Much Does a 75-Gallon Fish Tank Weigh When Filled?

By Hektor Jorgo Published March 27, 2030 Updated June 15, 2026 3 min read

  1. Direct Answer: Roughly 850-1000+ lbs All In
  2. Breaking Down the Total
  3. Floor and Stand Considerations
  4. Quick Reference

Quick Facts

Freshwater is roughly 8.34 lbs per gallon, so 75 gallons of water alone is roughly 625 lbs
Saltwater Weighs a Bit More
Roughly 8.5-8.6 lbs per gallon due to dissolved salts, putting 75 gallons around 640 lbs
Empty Tank Weight
A 75-gallon glass tank itself (without water) commonly weighs roughly 130-150 lbs depending on construction
Substrate Adds More
A few inches of substrate across a 75-gallon footprint can add roughly 75-150+ lbs
Rock and Decor
Live rock, large decorations, or hardscape can add significant additional weight beyond substrate
Total Filled Estimate
A fully set up 75-gallon tank often totals somewhere around 850-1000+ lbs depending on substrate and decor
Floor Loading

Bottom Line
Water dominates the total, but substrate, decor, and the tank itself are not negligible additions

A 75-gallon tank is a popular "stepping up" size — big enough for serious stocking options, small enough to fit through most doorways. What's easy to underestimate is how much heavier the finished tank is compared to "75 gallons of water," once everything else is added in.

Direct Answer: Roughly 850-1000+ lbs All In

A fully set-up 75-gallon tank typically weighs somewhere around 850-1000+ lbs total, combining: roughly 625 lbs of water (freshwater, at about 8.34 lbs/gallon — slightly more for saltwater), roughly 130-150 lbs for the empty glass tank itself, and roughly 100-200+ lbs of substrate, rock, and decor depending on depth and hardscape. Water dominates the total, but the other components together are far from negligible — easily 200-350+ lbs combined depending on setup. For most ground-floor placements on an appropriate stand, this is within normal residential floor capacity, though upper floors and older structures are worth a moment's thought.

Breaking Down the Total

Water: Freshwater is roughly 8.34 lbs per gallon, so 75 gallons ≈ 625 lbs. Saltwater is slightly denser — roughly 8.5-8.6 lbs/gallon, or about 640 lbs for 75 gallons. The difference (~15 lbs) is small relative to the whole.

The tank itself: An empty 75-gallon glass tank commonly weighs roughly 130-150 lbs, depending on glass thickness and construction (acrylic tanks of the same volume are often lighter, though less common at this size).

Substrate: This scales with footprint and depth. Our guide to sand quantity for a 20-gallon tank covers the calculation at a smaller scale — a 75-gallon tank has roughly 3-4x the footprint of a 20-gallon, so a few inches of substrate can put this component in the 75-150+ lb range. A laterite layer or a soil base under a sand cap adds to this further, though usually modestly compared to the main substrate layer.

Rock and decor: Highly variable — a sparsely decorated tank adds relatively little here, while a heavily rockscaped reef tank (live rock is notably dense) or a tank with large pieces of driftwood and stone can add a substantial amount on its own.

Floor and Stand Considerations

For a ground-floor placement on a properly rated stand, a 75-gallon tank is a very common setup and generally within what residential floors are designed to support — this isn't a tank size that typically raises structural alarm bells. A few things still worth keeping in mind:

  • How the load is distributed — a stand's legs concentrate weight at specific points rather than spreading it evenly, which matters more for delicate flooring
  • Upper-floor placement — span and joist considerations become more relevant than on a slab/ground floor
  • Older homes — less certainty about floor condition can be worth a quick look, especially for upper floors

None of this is a reason to avoid a 75-gallon tank — it's a reason to give the stand and placement a bit of thought, particularly positioning over joists for upper-floor setups. Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a 75-gallon fish tank weigh when fully filled and set up?

Somewhere around 850-1000+ lbs total, once you add up the water, the tank itself, the substrate, and any decor or rockwork. The biggest single component is the water — roughly 625 lbs for freshwater (at about 8.34 lbs/gallon) — but it's not the only thing being supported. The empty glass tank itself typically weighs roughly 130-150 lbs, and substrate plus decor can easily add another 100-200+ lbs depending on depth and what's in the tank. None of these individual numbers are surprising on their own, but added together they land at a total that's meaningfully more than '75 gallons of water' might suggest at first glance.

Does saltwater weigh meaningfully more than freshwater for the same volume?

Yes, but the difference is relatively small in absolute terms. Freshwater is roughly 8.34 lbs per gallon, while saltwater (depending on salinity) is roughly 8.5-8.6 lbs per gallon — the dissolved salts add a bit of density. For 75 gallons, that's roughly 625 lbs vs. around 640 lbs — a difference of about 15 lbs, which is small relative to the total filled weight of the setup. The water type matters far less to the total weight than substrate, rockwork (especially live rock in reef tanks, which can be substantial), and the tank/stand themselves.

How much extra weight do substrate and decorations actually add?

More than people often expect, especially for planted or heavily decorated setups. Substrate scales with both footprint and depth — our guide to how much sand a 20-gallon tank needs walks through that calculation at a smaller scale, and the same logic scales up: a 75-gallon tank has roughly 3-4x the footprint of a 20-gallon, so substrate in the 75-150+ lb range for a few inches of depth isn't unusual. On top of that, rocks, driftwood, and decorations — particularly dense materials like large rockwork or a thick laterite layer under the substrate — add further weight that's easy to underestimate piece by piece but adds up across an entire hardscape. None of this is dangerous in itself, but it's part of why 'water weight' alone understates the real number.

Do I need to worry about my floor supporting a 75-gallon tank?

For most ground-floor placements on a typical residential floor, a properly set-up 75-gallon tank on an appropriate stand is generally within what the floor structure can support — this is a very common tank size, and floors are designed with reasonable safety margins for distributed loads. That said, a few things are worth being mindful of: concentrated point loads (a stand with a few legs rather than a continuous base distributes weight differently than the same total weight spread evenly), upper-floor placement (where span and joist considerations become more relevant than on a slab or ground floor), and older homes with less certainty about floor condition. None of this means a 75-gallon tank is risky in a typical setup — it's more that, for upper floors or older structures specifically, it's a reasonable thing to think through (positioning over joists, an appropriately rated stand) rather than something to ignore entirely.
