The Red Jardini arowana (*Scleropages jardinii*) is one of the most striking freshwater fish in the trade — a sleek, large-finned predator with reflective scales and red-tinted fins. It's also one of the clearest examples of a fish whose **sale size and adult size are almost unrelated numbers**, and planning for the second number is the entire point of this guide.

## Appearance and Natural Range

Jardini arowana have an elongated, torpedo-shaped body, a large mouth set for surface and mid-water feeding, and long, flowing anal and dorsal fins that extend much of the length of the body. The "Red Jardini" designation describes a color morph with more pronounced red/orange tones in the fins and body, generally associated with populations from New Guinea, as distinct from the more silver-toned "Pearl Arowana" morph often linked to Australian populations. The species (*Scleropages jardinii*) is native to river systems in **northern Australia and New Guinea**.

## Tank Requirements

### Tank Size

**250+ gallons, with a tank length of 6 feet or more**, is the realistic target for an adult Red Jardini. This isn't just about water volume — arowana are strong, fast swimmers that need a long enough tank to turn around without difficulty, and a tank that's "big" by typical community-tank standards (say, the [55-gallon tank sometimes discussed for discus](/55-gallon-discus-tank/)) is dramatically undersized for an adult arowana.

### Aquascaping

**Open swimming space** is the priority — dense planting or heavy decor competes with the space an arowana needs to move. A **secure, well-fitted lid** is essential: arowana are well-known jumpers, particularly when startled, and can injure themselves on an open or poorly-secured tank top. Floating plants or dim lighting can help reduce startle responses for a fish that spends much of its time near the surface.

### Water Parameters

| Parameter | Target Range |
|---|---|
| Temperature | 75-86°F (24-30°C) — see our [arowana water temperature guide](/arowana-water-temperature/) |
| pH | 6.5-7.5 |
| Ammonia / Nitrite | 0 ppm |
| Nitrate | <20 ppm |
| Water hardness | Soft to moderately hard |

Given the size of tank required, **filtration and heating need to be sized for hundreds of gallons**, not scaled up casually from smaller-tank equipment — this is a meaningfully different equipment budget than most freshwater setups.

## Diet and Feeding

Red Jardini arowana are **carnivores** with a substantial appetite that grows alongside the fish:

- **Juveniles**: smaller meaty foods, appropriately-sized pellets formulated for carnivorous fish
- **Adults**: large carnivore pellets, and whole prey items (where appropriate and from safe sources) become part of the diet for many keepers

As with other large, fast-growing fish, **waste output scales with size** — the bioload principles discussed in our [pleco waste guide](/pleco-poop-information/) apply here in an amplified form, and filtration needs to be planned for an adult-sized, carnivorous fish's output, not a juvenile's.

## Tank Mates and Compatibility

Arowana are generally **not aggressive toward similarly-sized tank mates** in the sense of deliberately attacking them, but they are **predatory toward anything small enough to be viewed as food** — which, for an adult arowana, includes most typical community fish.

Appropriate tank mates are **large, robust species** that can share the substantial space an arowana setup requires without being at risk of becoming a meal. Large, hardy catfish are sometimes discussed in this context — species like [Colombian shark catfish](/colombian-shark-catfish/), which themselves need significant space (75+ gallons, groups of 3+) — though any such pairing only works if the tank is large enough to genuinely accommodate both species' adult needs, which in practice points back to the 250+ gallon range rather than a smaller "compromise" tank.

## Red Jardini vs. Other Arowana Varieties

"Arowana" covers several distinct species and color morphs in the trade, and names can be applied inconsistently between sellers — a pattern that comes up repeatedly in this hobby, as discussed for [stingray plecos](/stingray-pleco/) and ["Asian banjo catfish"](/how-to-care-for-asian-banjo-catfish/). For Jardini arowana specifically:

- **Scleropages jardinii** is the species — native to northern Australia and New Guinea
- **"Red Jardini"** generally describes individuals/populations with more red/orange in the fins and body
- **"Pearl Arowana"** is a commonly used name for more silver-toned Jardini, often associated with Australian-origin stock

The care requirements (tank size, water parameters, diet, jumping risk) are broadly the same across these color-morph distinctions — the morph affects appearance, not the fundamental space and care commitment.

## Common Health Issues

- **Injury from jumping/lid impacts** — a recurring issue for arowana given their jumping tendency, especially in tanks without secure lids
- **Stunting from inadequate tank size** — keeping an arowana in too small a tank for extended periods can affect growth and long-term health, separate from any single water quality issue
- **General water quality sensitivity** scaled to the fish's size — given the large bioload, lapses in filtration or maintenance have an outsized effect compared to smaller fish

## Quick Setup Checklist

- [ ] Tank: 250+ gallons, 6+ feet in length, for an adult
- [ ] Secure, well-fitted lid — arowana are strong jumpers
- [ ] Open swimming space, minimal dense decor
- [ ] Water temperature 75-86°F — see our [arowana water temperature guide](/arowana-water-temperature/)
- [ ] Diet: carnivore pellets scaled to size, whole prey items for adults
- [ ] Filtration and heating sized for hundreds of gallons, not scaled up from a smaller tank
- [ ] Tank mates: large, robust species only — no small community fish long-term
- [ ] Plan for a 10-20+ year commitment before acquiring
